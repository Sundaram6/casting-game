# Casting Office 3D — Narrative Satire Redesign

**Date:** 2026-06-28
**Status:** Draft
**Approach:** Full narrative redesign with multiple playable perspectives

---

## Overview

Transform Casting Office 3D from a meme/typing game into a narrative-driven serious satire about nepotism in the entertainment industry. The game explores how privilege, talent, and complicity intersect — told through three characters whose stories converge at a single casting call.

**Genre:** First-person narrative exploration
**Tone:** Serious satire — critical but not preachy. Lets the player draw conclusions.
**Visual style:** Stylized realism (Wes Anderson / Life is Strange aesthetic)
**Target length:** 45-90 minutes total across all three chapters
**Platform:** Browser (WebGL), with mobile support

---

## Story: "Casting Call"

### Setting

A casting office complex in Andheri West, Mumbai — the heart of the Hindi film industry. The building sits on a busy lane near Meera Road, surrounded by production houses, audition studios, and the chaos of Mumbai. Auto-rickshaws hum outside, chai wallahs serve tea on the corner, and every billboard advertises a film. The plaza, offices, and surrounding streets form the game's map. Same locations, different feelings depending on who you're playing.

**Key locations:**
- **Raksh Chhabra Casting** — The main casting office. Sleek, intimidating, glass doors. Posters of every major film on the walls. This is where careers are made.
- **Sonali Kapoor Associates** — A smaller, warmer casting office. More independent films, more genuine interest in talent. But still part of the machine.
- **Dhamaka Productions** — Arjun's father's production house. Gold-tinted signage, marble lobby, security guards who know every star kid by name.
- **The waiting room** — A purgatory of plastic chairs, outdated posters, and the smell of chai. Everyone here is waiting. Some have been waiting for years.
- **Sundaram's PG accommodation** — A cramped paying-guest room in Andheri. Thin walls, a shared bathroom, a single window overlooking a construction site. He shares it with two other struggling actors.
- **Arjun's apartment** — A high-rise in Bandra with a sea view. Minimalist furniture, a home gym, a script on the coffee table with notes in his father's handwriting.
- **Rekha's office** — A cluttered room in the casting complex. Three decades of scripts, headshots, and rejection letters. A framed photo of her with a young Adivasi actress who later disappeared — she fought for her, and she lost.

### Three Characters

#### 1. Sundaram Sharma (The Outsider)

**Background:** Late 20s, from Patna, Bihar. A theater artist who spent 8 years performing in small auditoriums, street plays, and Nukkad Natak across Bihar and Jharkhand. Won a state-level acting competition that caught the attention of a Mumbai-based casting assistant. Sold his father's cycle and borrowed money from a college friend to buy a one-way train ticket to Mumbai. Staying in a PG in Andheri with two other struggling actors. He speaks fluent Hindi, English, and Bhojpuri — and switches between them depending on who he's talking to. In Bhojpuri, he's funnier, warmer, more himself. In English, he's precise, careful, performing respectability.

**Chapter arc:**
- Arrives in Mumbai after a 16-hour train journey. The city is everything and nothing like he imagined.
- Navigates to the casting office using a hand-drawn map and a auto-rickshaw driver who overcharges him.
- In the waiting room, he's surrounded by actors who have agents, portfolios, and connections. He has a creased headshot from a local photographer and a monologue from a play he did in Patna.
- Witnesses the system firsthand: a famous insider's nephew walks in and gets called in immediately. Sundaram has been waiting for 3 hours.
- The audition itself: he performs a monologue from a Hindi play. His voice fills the room. He uses all three languages — Hindi for the emotion, English for the technical precision, Bhojpuri for the raw, unpolished truth. The casting director is moved. Then watches a less talented but connected actor get the part.
- Realization: talent was never the point

**Language usage:**
- **Hindi:** His primary language. Used for auditions, formal conversations, emotional scenes.
- **English:** Used when he's trying to be taken seriously. Slightly formal, textbook-flavored but fluent.
- **Bhojpuri:** His mother tongue. Used in flashbacks to Bihar, when he's tired and lets his guard down, in moments of genuine emotion. The player sees Bhojpuri dialogue as subtitles but hears the warmth in his voice.

**Gameplay focus:** Exploration, observation, environmental storytelling. Sundaram's chapter is about *seeing* the industry for the first time.

#### 2. Arjun Malhotra (The Nepo Kid)

**Background:** Early 20s, son of Vikram Malhotra — one of Bollywood's most powerful producers (Dhamaka Productions). Grew up on film sets, knows every crew member by name. Has wanted to act since childhood. Genuinely loves the craft — he's watched every Satyajit Ray film, can recite dialogues from Manoj Bajpayee's films, and genuinely respects the art. But he's never had to fight for anything. Lives in a luxury apartment in Bandra, drives a Jeep Compass, has an agent (his father's friend) on speed dial. His Instagram has 2 million followers. He's never auditioned for anything in his life.

**Chapter arc:**
- Wakes up in his Bandra apartment. Gets a call from his father's assistant: "Vikram ji says the Raksh Chhabra meeting is confirmed. You got the part."
- Goes to the casting office in his father's Audi. People recognize him, greet him warmly. "Arjun bhai! Your father is a legend."
- Encounters Sundaram in the waiting room. Sees genuine talent, raw and unpolished. Feels something he can't name — envy? guilt? respect?
- The audition: he does well, but knows in his heart it wasn't because he was the best. The casting director (Raksh) is warm but professional. "You have the look. Your father's instincts are always right."
- Confrontation with his father over dinner at a Bandra restaurant: "I wanted to earn it." Vikram's response, in Hindi: "Tere baap ne mehnat ki hai taaki tereko mehnat na karni pade. Yahi hai industry ka system." (Your father worked hard so you wouldn't have to. That's how this industry works.)
- Realization: his success has a cost, and someone else paid it

**Language usage:**
- **Hindi:** His comfort language. Used with family, friends, casual settings. Fluent, Mumbai-flavored.
- **English:** Used in professional settings, interviews, social media. Slightly performative.
- **Occasional Bhojpuri/Hindi mix:** When he meets Sundaram and tries to connect, awkwardly using Bhojpuri phrases he half-remembers from a film shoot in Bihar.

**Gameplay focus:** Dialogue choices, relationship dynamics. Arjun's chapter is about *privilege and guilt*.

#### 3. Rekha Iyer (The Gatekeeper)

**Background:** Late 50s. A veteran casting director who has worked in the Hindi film industry for 30 years. Started as an assistant to a legendary casting director in the 90s, learned the craft when Bollywood was still about talent and auditions. She's cast National Award winners and complete failures. She discovered three major stars in their debut films. She believes in talent, but she also believes in "practical considerations" — the calls she gets from producers, the deals made at Filmfare parties, the debt she owes to people who helped her career. She's known as "the honest one" in the industry, which means she compromises slightly less than everyone else.

**Chapter arc:**
- Morning routine in her Juhu flat: filter coffee, a stack of scripts, the weight of another day. Her phone buzzes with messages from producers, agents, assistants.
- Reviews audition tapes at her desk. Sundaram's tape is brilliant. Arjun's is... fine. She's seen this before.
- Phone call from Vikram Malhotra (Arjun's father): "Rekha ji, we're going with Arjun. The director agrees." Rekha pushes back — "There's a boy from Bihar, he was extraordinary" — then relents. She always relents.
- Flashback: a younger Rekha, early in her career, fighting for an unknown actor (an Adivasi girl from Jharkhand) against a producer's nephew. She lost that fight. The girl never acted again.
- Meeting with Sundaram after his audition. She sees his potential. She also sees his disappointment. She wants to tell him the truth — that it's not about talent — but she can't. She's part of this machine.
- Realization: she's been making the same phone call for 30 years, and she's tired

**Language usage:**
- **Hindi:** Her primary language. Fluent, polished Mumbai Hindi with occasional Marathi words (she grew up in Maharashtra).
- **English:** Used in professional settings, with international co-productions. Refined, articulate.
- **Tamil:** She occasionally switches to Tamil when she's emotional — she's half-Tamil, half-Marathi, and Tamil was her mother's language.

**Gameplay focus:** Decision-making, moral compromise. Rekha's chapter is about *complicity*.

All three characters are at Raksh Chhabra Casting for the same role — a lead in a Dhamaka Productions film. The player experiences the same 15-minute window from three perspectives. Key moments:

## Gameplay Systems

### Dialogue System

- **Branching conversations** with NPCs (other actors, agents, assistants, the casting director)
- Dialogue in **Hindi, English, and Hinglish** — the natural code-switching of Mumbai
- Player chooses from 2-3 response options per conversation beat
- No "correct" choices — every option reveals character and shifts relationships
- **Relationship tracker** (hidden or visible) affects available dialogue and story beats
- NPCs remember what you said and reference it later
- **Bhojpuri dialogue** appears in Sundaram's chapter — subtitle option for non-speakers
- Silence is also a choice — sometimes saying nothing says everything

### Environmental Storytelling

The world tells stories without exposition:

- **Scripts and papers** on desks — Hindi film scripts with highlighted dialogues, audition notes, rejection letters (some in Hindi, some in English)
- **Office decor** — Filmfare and IIFA awards on walls (whose?), family photos with famous faces, motivational posters ("Hard work beats talent when talent doesn't work hard" — ironic given the game's theme)
- **Overheard conversations** — NPCs discuss industry gossip: "Did you hear? Vikram Malhotra's son got the Dhamaka film." / "Arre, he's a good actor na?" / "Good actor? He has 2 million Instagram followers, that's what he has."
- **WhatsApp messages** — found on phones or tablets in the environment: "Bhai, Rekha ma'am ka number hai kya?" / "Vikram ji ne bola hai audition mat do, seedha meeting karo"
- **Physical details** — Chai stains on scripts, worn carpet paths, a door that doesn't close properly, a fan that wobbles
- **Cultural details** — A chai wallah's stall visible through the window, auto-rickshaws passing by, a poster for a nearby Navratri celebration, a dog sleeping in the hallway

### Memory/Flashback System

Each character can "remember" key moments:

- Activated by environmental triggers (a familiar face, a specific office, a sound, a smell — chai, old scripts, a familiar song)
- Brief, controllable vignettes — 30-60 seconds each
- Recontextualize current events:
  - Sundaram: A flashback to his father's small shop in Patna. His mother saying, "Beta, Mumbai jaake bada ban" (Son, go to Mumbai and become someone big). The weight of that expectation.
  - Arjun: A flashback to a film set as a child. His father telling a director, "Arjun ko role do. Woh talented hai." (Give Arjun the role. He's talented.) The director's face — the smile that says everything.
  - Rekha: A flashback to 1998. A young Adivasi actress from Jharkhand, sitting in this same waiting room. Rekha fought for her. The producer said no. Rekha let it go. She never saw the actress again.
- Not mandatory — player can explore at their own pace

### The Audition Scene

The climax of the game. Played three times:

1. **Sundaram's version:** He performs a monologue from a Hindi play he did in Patna. He starts in Hindi, switches to English for a moment of precision, then drops into Bhojpuri for the raw, emotional truth. The room is silent. He nails it. Cut to: the casting assistant telling him, "Very good, Sundaram ji. We'll let you know." He sees Arjun walk out of the next room with a smile.
2. **Arjun's version:** He performs a prepared monologue from a Hindi film. He's nervous but prepared. The casting director (Raksh) is kind but distant. "You have the look, Arjun ji. Your father's instincts are always right." He does well enough. Cut to: his father's call. "Good job, beta. Dhamaka mein tera scene hai." (Good job, son. Your scene is in Dhamaka.)
3. **Rekha's version:** She watches both tapes side by side on her laptop. Sundaram's tape: raw, brilliant, three languages weaving together like a tapestry. Arjun's tape: competent, safe, the product of privilege. Her face tells the story. She picks up the phone. She makes the call she always makes: "Vikram ji, Arjun is confirmed." She puts the phone down. She stares at Sundaram's tape. She closes the laptop.

No minigame. No typing. Just performance and consequence.

### No Fail State

- Every path leads somewhere meaningful
- No "game over" — the story continues regardless of choices
- The tragedy/satire is the point, not winning or losing
- Multiple endings per character based on dialogue choices, but all are thematically consistent

## Visual Direction: Stylized Realism

### Color Grading

| Character | Palette | Mood |
|-----------|---------|------|
| Sundaram | Warm golds, ambers, hopeful tones — the colors of a Bihar morning | Nostalgia, hope, gradual desaturation as reality sets in |
| Arjun | Cool whites, steel blues, sterile luxury — the colors of a Bandra high-rise | Oppressive perfection, emotional distance |
| Rekha | Muted greys, beiges, fluorescent wash — the colors of a long career | Exhaustion, routine, the color of compromise |

### Lighting Design

- **Naturalistic but art-directed** — every light source has purpose
- **Harsh fluorescent** for office interiors and auditions (institutional, unflattering — the light of Indian offices)
- **Golden hour** for Sundaram's arrival and outdoor scenes (hope, the warmth of a Mumbai sunset)
- **Blue hour** for Rekha's late nights (melancholy, the weight of decisions)
- **Mixed lighting** for Arjun — luxury warm tones that feel performative (the soft lighting of wealth)
- **Chai stall warmth** — the orange glow of a tea stall visible through the casting office window, a constant reminder of the world outside

### Environment Art

- The casting office as the central hub — same space, different emotional feel per character
- **Waiting room:** Film magazines (Filmfare, Stardust), outdated headshots, a TV playing an old Bollywood film, a chai stall visible through the window, a poster for "Raksh Chhabra Casting — Launching careers since 1995"
- **Audition room:** Harsh light, a camera on a tripod, a chair, a reading desk with a water bottle. A poster of a famous actor who was discovered here.
- **Hallway:** Posters of past productions — some with familiar faces. A trophy case. A sign: "No cell phones in the audition room."
- **Mumbai cityscape** visible through windows — stylized, not photorealistic. Palm trees, construction cranes, the haze of monsoon season, a distant view of the Sea Link.
- **Small details:** Chai stains on scripts, a half-eaten vada pav in a wrapper, a pen with "Dhamaka Productions" printed on it, a script with lines highlighted in three different colors, a newspaper clipping about nepotism in Bollywood

### Character Design

- Semi-realistic proportions (not cartoonish, not photorealistic)
- Expressive faces — the game depends on reading emotion
- Distinct visual identity for each character:
  - Sundaram: Simple kurta-shirt, worn jeans, a well-worn backpack from Patna, an audition outfit he clearly saved up for (a formal shirt pressed the night before). A small tilak on his forehead. Calloused hands from years of stage work.
  - Arjun: Designer casual — crisp white sneakers, a well-fitted jacket, Ray-Ban sunglasses pushed up on his head. Nothing out of place. A watch that costs more than Sundaram's PG room.
  - Rekha: Professional but tired — reading glasses on a chain, a cotton saree or a practical kurta, a coffee-stained script in her hand. Simple gold jewelry. The look of someone who has seen everything and is tired of most of it.

## Sound Design

### Ambient Soundscapes

| Location | Sounds |
|----------|--------|
| Casting office lobby | Air conditioning hum, distant phones, muffled Hindi conversations, chai being poured, a fan whirring |
| Audition room | Silence, a ticking clock, the scratch of a pen on paper, the distant sound of traffic |
| Hallway | Footsteps on tile, a door closing, someone practicing dialogues in Hindi |
| Mumbai streets | Auto-rickshaw horns, distant Bollywood music from a shop, construction noise, a dog barking, a chai wallah calling out |
| Sundaram's PG | A ceiling fan wobbling, thin walls (a neighbor's TV playing a cricket match), traffic outside, a phone ringing in another room |
| Arjun's apartment | City hum through glass, a sleek door closing, silence, a playlist playing softly in the background |

### Music

- **Minimal, piano and harmonium-driven score.** No Bollywood bombast. The irony is intentional.
- **No music during dialogue** — let silence and voice carry the weight
- **Music only for transitions and emotional peaks:**
  - Sundaram's arrival in Mumbai: a simple, hopeful harmonium melody — the sound of a Bihar morning
  - The audition: no music. Just the sound of breathing and a distant auto-rickshaw.
  - Rekha's flashback: a distant, music-box version of a 90s Bollywood song — the music of her youth
  - The ending: a single piano note held over the credits, then silence
- **Source music** (diegetic): A radio in the casting office playing soft background Bollywood instrumental. A phone ringtone. A neighbor's music through thin walls.

### Voice Acting

- Full voice acting for all three protagonists and key NPCs
- **Trilingual dialogue** — Hindi, English, and Bhojpuri (for Sundaram's chapter)
- Authentic speech patterns:
  - Sundaram: Hindi with a Bhojpuri accent, precise English, switches to Bhojpuri when emotional or talking to family
  - Arjun: Polished Mumbai Hindi, fluent English, occasional Marathi phrases (from growing up in Maharashtra)
  - Rekha: Refined Hindi with occasional Tamil words, articulate English, the speech of someone who's spent 30 years in the industry
- **Subtitles:** All dialogue subtitled. Bhojpuri and Marathi lines have additional context subtitles.
- **Silence as a tool** — some moments play out without dialogue

### Foley

- Script pages turning (Hindi and English scripts)
- Footsteps on different surfaces (tile, carpet, concrete, the thin carpet of a PG room)
- Chai being poured, a kulhar (clay cup) being set down
- Door handles, lock clicks
- A pen clicking nervously
- The sound of a phone being picked up and put down
- An auto-rickshaw engine idling outside
- A ceiling fan wobbling
- The crackle of a chai stall stove

## UI Design

### Narrative UI (Minimal)

- **No HUD during exploration** — full immersion
- **Dialogue system:** Character portraits with text. Clean, minimal. Choice indicators (not numbered — that feels gamey).
- **Journal:** Auto-populated with discovered story details. Organized by character. Optional — never forced on the player.
- **Character switcher:** When switching perspectives, a brief title card with the character's name and a single line of context in Hindi and English ("3 घंटे पहले" / "3 hours earlier" / "इधर" / "Meanwhile")

### Transition Design

- **Character switches** are narrative events, not menu selections
- Fade to black. Title card in Hindi and English. Fade in on new character.
- Title cards example:
  - "तीन घंटे पहले" / "Three hours earlier"
  - "इधर" / "Meanwhile"
  - "अगली सुबह" / "The next morning"
  - "उसी शाम" / "That evening"
- Sometimes triggered by story beats (Sundaram's audition ends → cut to Arjun's morning)
- Sometimes player-triggered (at save points / chapter breaks)

## Scope & Phasing

This is a significant project. Recommended phasing:

### Phase 1: Core Narrative Framework
- Character switching mechanic
- Dialogue engine
- Environmental interaction (examine objects)
- One character's full chapter (Sundaram) as proof of concept
- Basic ambient sound

### Phase 2: Second Character
- Arjun's chapter
- Flashback system
- Relationship tracker
- Enhanced environmental storytelling

### Phase 3: Third Character + Polish
- Rekha's chapter
- Journal system
- Color grading per character
- Full sound design (music, foley, voice)
- Mobile optimization

### Phase 4: Convergence + Release
- The shared audition scene from 3 perspectives
- Ending sequences
- Performance optimization
- Testing and polish

## Success Criteria

1. **Emotional impact:** Player feels something — discomfort, empathy, reflection — by the end
2. **Narrative clarity:** Each character's story is understandable without reading the others first
3. **Visual coherence:** The art direction serves the story, not just aesthetics
4. **Sound immersion:** The soundscape makes the world feel real without overwhelming
5. **Performance:** Smooth 30+ FPS on desktop, 20+ FPS on mobile
6. **Accessibility:** Subtitles, scalable text, keyboard-only navigation option

## Risks & Mitigations

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|--------|------------|
| Scope creep | Never finishes | Strict phasing. Phase 1 is the MVP — ship it, then expand. |
| Voice acting cost | Budget blow | Start with high-quality TTS or volunteer actors. Can be replaced later. |
| Narrative branching complexity | Exponential dialogue trees | Keep branches shallow — 2-3 choices per conversation, not 10. Converge quickly. |
| Performance with 3D + audio | Laggy on mobile | Aggressive LOD, audio compression, adaptive quality settings |
| Moral complexity feels preachy | Player disengages | Never tell the player what to think. Show, don't tell. Let silence speak. |
